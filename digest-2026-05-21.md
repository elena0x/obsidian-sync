### Roblox Product Lead Peter Yang on the Future of Work in an AI-Agent-First World
Peter Yang, who leads product at Roblox and runs a newsletter with 140K+ readers, shared five big takeaways from a conversation with a16z GP Anish Acharya about how AI agents are reshaping knowledge work:

1. **Coding will eat all knowledge work** — Writing docs, building slides, pulling analytics: Yang now gets the first 80% done through AI coding agents, then manually polishes the last 20%. He never starts from zero anymore.
2. **Small teams will outperform large orgs** — Both Yang and Acharya remember sitting in 3-hour OKR meetings thinking "this is wasting my life." This generation's founders know to stay tiny on purpose. 2-3 person product teams with a swarm of agents will replace overstaffed orgs.
3. **Apps for completing tasks will shrink** — Since wiring up Google Workspace, Mercury, and other APIs to his OpenClaw, Yang barely opens those apps anymore. But he still scrolls X every day. Apps that entertain you will outlast the ones you open to get stuff done.
4. **Personal agents that understand us deeply** — Yang was on a walk with his OpenClaw when it said: "You keep talking about your career and business. Just remember your kids are 7 and 4. They're going to grow up soon — optimize for spending time with them instead." A wake-up call he didn't expect.
5. **Human ambition has no ceiling** — The shape of the economy is changing, not shrinking. We'll see more one-person companies and small teams in light of ongoing big-tech layoffs. As someone tweeted recently, "The job market is so bad I have no choice but to pursue my dreams."

### Box CEO Aaron Levie: AI Agents Haven't Changed the Nature of Work — Just Shifted It Up a Layer
Levie pushed back on the idea that agents eliminate work entirely. When agents do work for you, the work just moves up a layer of abstraction: figuring out what to tell the agent, ensuring proper instructions, getting needed context, intervening when it goes off task, reviewing the final output, and incorporating it into something else. Any one of these components being off and "poof you will have useless work product." Even as agents complete longer-horizon tasks, planning and review stages don't go away — in fact, the longer the task, the more they take up. "You are now the editor. A manager. A producer. The taste matters, understanding the craft matters, and the skills ultimately matter."

### Latent Space: Mistral's Voxtral TTS, Forge, Leanstral & What's Next for Mistral 4

**The Takeaway:** Mistral is betting that specialized, efficient models beat giant generalists — and they're backing it up with open weights, aggressive cost efficiency, and a roadmap toward full-duplex voice agents.

Mistral Chief Scientist Guillaume Lample and Audio Research Lead Pavan Kumar Reddy joined the Latent Space podcast to unpack three major releases: Voxtral TTS (their first open-weights speech generation model), Mistral Small (a merged multimodal model), and Leanstral (their first foray into formal mathematical proving).

On **Voxtral TTS**, the team revealed a novel architecture: an autoregressive flow matching model with an in-house neural audio codec that compresses audio into 12.5 Hz latent tokens (semantic + acoustic). Unlike competitors that use depth transformers requiring K autoregressive steps per frame, Mistral's flow matching approach cuts inference to just 4-16 steps — a major latency win for real-time voice agents. The 3B model runs on the Ministral backbone, supports 9 languages, and is priced at "a fraction" of closed competitors. Pavan noted that audio generation has more entropy than text: even the same word in the same voice can be inflected countless ways, making distribution-based approaches like flow matching more natural than discrete token prediction.

On **Mistral Small**, Guillaume explained their "merge then optimize" strategy: individual teams build specialized capabilities (coding, vision, audio, function calling), then merge them into a single sparse MoE with only 6B active parameters but 256K context. He pushed back on the "one giant omni model" vision, arguing that if you just need transcription, a 1B or 3B specialized model performs essentially the same at vastly lower cost.

On **Leanstral**, Guillaume revealed a small internal team working on formal mathematical proving using Lean. The motivation: most reasoning problems lack easy verification ("prove this function is continuous" has no reference answer to compare against). Lean changes this — if it compiles, it's correct. The team sees this as foundational not just for math but for software verification, an industry currently limited to aerospace and life-critical systems but poised to expand dramatically with coding agents.

**Key quote from Guillaume:** "We really don't want to be living in a world where the smartest model, the best models are only behind closed doors, only accessible to a few companies that have the power to decide who can use them or not. It's a scary future we don't want to live in."
